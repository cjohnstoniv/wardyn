The scoring campaign's evidence lived in a gitignored scratch tree and would have
died with the branch. This is the durable record: what was measured, how, what it
found, and what is still open. It spans four rounds; the numbers below are the
**final** state, with the arc called out where a dimension moved.

Round 3's goal was every dimension ≥ 80; it landed at composite 86 with eight

different kind of round: the lever was no longer lifting raw scores but
**clearing the open findings that pinned the ceilings** — plus fixing the
residuals behind the two dimensions whose ceiling was already 100
(documentation, supply chain). The weighted composite is 92.14, floored to
**92**. Security and Reliability (the double-weighted pair) remain honestly
ceiling-bound at 85 — see the residuals section.

## How the score works

`score = min(raw, ceiling)` per dimension; composite = weighted mean (security and
reliability count double; Σw = 14).

- **raw** = `(pass + 0.5·partial) / (pass + partial + fail)` over that dimension's
  criteria verdicts.
- **ceiling** = the worst OPEN finding above low-confidence in that dimension:
  crit→35, high→60, med→85, none→100.
- Any open high-confidence HIGH clamps the whole composite to ≤74.

The composite is **92** and un-clamped: every high-confidence HIGH is
verified-resolved (the gate is lifted). Six dimensions are clean at 100
(architecture, testing, CLI-UX, UI+a11y, supply chain, maintainability) —
their open-MED pools were cleared, so the raw score shows through an
un-clamped ceiling. DX (94) and performance (90) are raw-bound under a
ceiling of 100. Security and reliability are **ceiling-bound at 85**: their
raw is 96, but each still carries a pool of open medium-severity findings
(66 and 124 respectively) whose worst pins the ceiling. Documentation (83)
and build/release/CI (83) are raw-bound below their ceilings on honestly
partial criteria (see residuals).

## What makes a claim count (unchanged across all three rounds)

- **Resolution is machine-verified.** A finding is credited only when its
  `resolved:{sha,test}` re-proves on every scorer run: the commit must be an
  ancestor of the scored ref *and* the named test must still exist in the tree. An
  unbacked marker is silently ignored — it can under-count, never fake.
- **Verdicts move only by blind re-assessment.** Reviewers read the current code
  with no prior verdicts and no target score. Hand-editing a verdict is forbidden.
  Round 3's numbers came from 30 blind lane re-assessments across four waves;
  round 4 added **18 more** (13 lanes after the fix waves, 4 re-judged after
  fixing what that pass itself found, and 1 final re-judge after the last fix).
- **Round 4's new instrument: the reconciliation sweep.** Medium-severity
  findings had never been groomed — fixes from earlier rounds resolved many
  without anyone recording it (clearing *some* MEDs never moved a ceiling, so
  markers were only ever added for HIGHs). Round 4 sent **38 verifier agents
  over all 237 unique open MEDs**; a finding could be marked resolved only with
  the fixing commit *and* a still-present counterfactual test, an adversarial
  audit re-checked a 20% sample (0 rejections), and the scorer re-proves every
  marker from git on every run — an unprovable claim is silently ignored. The
  sweep surfaced 41 already-fixed findings; everything else stayed open.
- **Fixes carry counterfactual tests.** Each non-trivial fix was verified by a
  test that fails if the fix is reverted (e.g. removing the orchestrator's
  restore-from-store block fails the multi-substrate kill-switch recovery test;
  removing the docker `register.go` init fails the substrate-registry resolve).

## The honest dip, and why the final number is trustworthy

The middle of round 3 is the most important part of the record. After the ten
groups above landed, a **comprehensive 15-lane blind re-assessment at full
coverage** — security and reliability judged against the crown review's expanded
17-criterion checklists — did two things at once:

1. It moved Security and Reliability **82 → 85** (the full-coverage pass credited
   the fixes the earlier structural-only pass could not see), and surfaced **zero
   new CRIT or HIGH** findings across the whole codebase.
2. Being stricter than the prior round on the user-facing lanes, it **dropped
   CLI-UX 100 → 80 and DX/onboarding 85 → 78**, and surfaced ~24 real LOW defects
   — including a **doubled `wardyn: wardyn:` error prefix that this round's own SDK
   migration had introduced**.

The composite dipped to 84. Rather than merge the friendlier earlier verdicts and
score around it, round 3 **fixed the real defects the adversarial pass found**
(the S3 commits) and then re-judged the affected lanes on the fixed tree. That
re-judge — blind, no target — returned CLI-UX to 100 and DX to 85 and lifted
Build/CI to 85, for a final composite of **86**. The number is trustworthy
*because* it survived being driven down first.

## The coverage correction (S1 — no score changed)

Security read 55% coverage going into round 3, which invited the reading that half
its criteria were unevaluated. Investigation showed the 55% was a **denominator
artifact**: 8 security lanes × a shared 10-criterion checklist = 80 rows, of which
36 were correctly marked "not-evaluated — owned by lane X" (scope partitions). The
scorer now measures coverage over **unique criteria with an owning-lane verdict**,
and drops a stale hardcoded "capped" set for lanes the crown review has since
deep-reviewed. This is bookkeeping honesty, not score-gaming: it was verified by
diffing `scores.json` before and after — **every dimension's score/raw/ceiling and
the composite are byte-identical; only the printed coverage moved** (security
55→100, reliability 68→100, architecture 90→100). Coverage never enters the score.

## A note on who reviewed this round

The plan pinned Fable as the reviewer/finalizer for every diff and re-assessment.
Partway through round 3 the account switched and Fable became unavailable ("usage
credits are required for this model"). Per the standing rule that a reviewer is
**Opus or Fable, never the implementing tier**, every Fable call fell back to
**Opus**, and the fallback was recorded rather than papered over.

Then Fable came back online for a final validation pass, and it did exactly what a
pinned reviewer should: it **confirmed all four S3 fix commits** (spinning up a
live Postgres to prove migration 0022's concurrent-dedup under `-race`),
**independently re-judged the four recovery lanes blind**, and audited this
document against the scorer (SHIP, no discrepancy). Its lane re-judge was
**stricter than Opus's** — it held Build/release/CI at 83 rather than 85 — and
that stricter verdict is the one that stands here. Taking the pinned reviewer's
*less* favorable number over the fallback's is the whole point of the method. No
review was done by the model that wrote the code under review.

In round 4 Fable was available throughout: every implementation diff was
Fable-reviewed before commit (two lanes were sent back with mustFix lists and
re-reviewed after fix-up — one review caught a tidy-check recipe that would have
silently destroyed uncommitted `go.mod` edits), all 18 blind lane
re-assessments were Fable with zero Opus fallbacks, and the reconciliation
sweep's resolved-markers were adversarially audit-sampled by Fable (0
rejections). One round-4 task was **blocked by the automation safety
classifier** — enabling GitHub branch protection is an admin-level outward
write — and per standing policy it was not retried; the exact commands now live
in `RELEASING.md` for an operator.

## Honest residuals (open, disclosed, not credited)

- **Security and reliability are ceiling-bound at 85.** Their raw score is 96,
  but 66 and 124 medium-severity findings remain open after the reconciliation
  sweep — real engineering, not bookkeeping: secret-store key versioning, a
  durable egress-decision spool, proxy emit-path allocation churn
  (X004/X013), per-runtime MITM-CA installs, exec-less-substrate lifecycle
  edges. Clearing a pool's worst finding is what lifts its ceiling; nothing
  short of that honestly can.
- **Helm cannot produce a working deploy.** No published `wardynd` image and no
  Kubernetes runner driver (both v0.5). The from-source **compose** path works and
  is CI-validated; the helm gap is disclosed in `deploy/helm` and holds the
  Build/CI "working deploy" criterion at partial.
- **Two GitHub-side settings await an operator.** The three pre-alpha releases
  are still flagged non-prerelease, and `main` has no branch protection. Both
  are admin-level outward writes the automation safety classifier blocked (not
  retried, by policy); `RELEASING.md` § "One-time repo settings" carries the
  exact commands. Running them flips the release criterion to pass and makes
  CONTRIBUTING's check list a true server-side merge block.
- **Quickstart runnability is asserted, not machine-enforced.** `scripts/setup.sh`
  is statically consistent with the docs, but no committed CI gate runs
  `make setup` end-to-end; the docs-truth and config-env criteria stay partial
  for it. (The nightly e2e does boot the compose stack — the gap is `make
  setup` itself.)
- **Dark-theme contrast is comment-asserted, not test-enforced** — the
  committed WCAG-AA guard recomputes light-theme ratios only.
- **Size gates are green but not zero-residual.** Four 1000+-line files (plus
  two UI screens) remain on the frozen allowlist, each justified in
  ARCHITECTURE.md; `compose.go` and `store.go` left the list this round by
  being genuinely decomposed.
- **`GO-2026-5932`** (x/crypto/openpgp, unmaintained, no fix) is accepted:
  the flagged subpackage is never imported (both entry paths documented in
  SECURITY.md); `govulncheck` reports 0 called-path vulnerabilities on both
  tag sets. The SBOM is generated (syft, CycloneDX) but artifact **signing is
  a disclosed stub**.
